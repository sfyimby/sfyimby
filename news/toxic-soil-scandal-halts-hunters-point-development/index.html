Toxic Soil Scandal Halts Hunters Point Development

Diego Aguilar Canabal
Thursday, September 22, 2016

Developers at Lennar Urban received a crushing blow to their plans to develop 12,000 new homes and office space at the Hunters Point Shipyard, from none other than the Environmental Protection Agency. The EPA ordered the Mayor’s Office of Housing and the U.S. Navy to halt the transfer of land in the former shipyard after soil tests conducted by Tetra Tech were found to have been falsified.

The former naval yard housed a military research facility for nuclear weapons, leaving behind deadly radioactive soil when it closed in 1994. After two decades of cleanup and even some new apartments filling up with tenants, the Navy has admitted that its contractor, Tetra Tech, took soil samples from a clean site and passed them off as samples from the once-contaminated shipyard. The accusations were corroborated by the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission. Indeed, as Curbed SF points out, Tetra Tech quite literally covered up the dirt.

The federal agency’s understandable caution still provoked concern among city officials struggling to mitigate effects of the regional housing shortage. Mayor Ed Lee and Supervisor Malia Cohen, whose district encompasses Hunters Point, sent a letter to the EPA requesting a (“confidential if necessary”) briefing so they could understand just how long an investigation of Tetra Tech’s soil analysis could take. The 12,000 total units now stalled in the pipeline make up more than 10% of San Francisco’s estimated housing need. Currently, plans call for 1,400 new homes to be completed by 2018.

For the thousands of people struggling to find a home in San Francisco, the EPA’s delay spells a stark choice between commuting from distant suburbs, or risking a miniature Chernobyl in their own back yard.
